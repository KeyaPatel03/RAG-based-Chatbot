DECIDE WHAT'S MOST IMPORTANT TO YOU

Everyone's goals and interests are unique, so it's important to identify what matters to you when researching colleges. Take a moment to reflect on your priorities and be honest with yourself–what your
family
wants in a school may not necessarily align with what you want. This is a great opportunity to get to know yourself better to consider what you want from your college experience.

For example, here are some things to consider as you reflect on your ideal college:

• Think about the location of the college. Do you plan to live at home? Do you want to live on campus but be within a few hours from home? Does going to a school in a different state sound like the best option for you?
• How do extracurricular activities, like clubs or sports, factor into your decision?
• Make sure to research the size of the school and its classes. Do you want a small professor to student ratio? Is a bigger school with bigger class sizes more ideal?
• Is it important for your school to share your religious and/or social awareness values? How much will these requirements weigh on your decision to apply?

USE THE RIGHT SEARCH TOOLS

To begin your research, we recommend using three resources. The
U.S. Department of Education's College Scorecard tool
and
College Board's BigFuture platform
both provide information about colleges' graduation rates, average annual costs, and student body demographics. If financial fit is a priority for you,
uAspire’s list of financially safer schools
can also help in your search.

If you have specific schools in mind, use their websites to research academic factors like
majors
or
minors
offered, classes offered and their descriptions, and professor profiles. To get a better sense of the college's culture, values, and community, you can also follow their social media accounts to see what's happening on and around campus. Using these tools will allow you to get a full picture of each college before
deciding where to apply
.

ORGANIZE YOUR LIST AND KEEP IT MANAGEABLE

When you begin your college search, you may already have a long list of schools–and that’s okay! It’s completely normal to start with a list of 20-30 colleges that meet your criteria before narrowing it down as you go deeper into your research. Once you have your list, categorize them into
reach, match,
and
solid
schools:

• Reach schools are the ones you dream of going to, but are a bit more competitive to get into due to things like lower acceptance rates, minimum required GPAs or test scores, or even high costs of tuition.
• Match schools are schools you feel confident about getting accepted to, even if they’re not necessarily at the very top of your list.
• Solid schools are ones you have a strong chance of getting admitted to. Your test scores, GPA, or coursework typically match those of other incoming students at these schools.
Once everything is categorized, consider narrowing your list down to 10-15 colleges, with a few schools in each category to keep your options open.
Organizing your list
and keeping it manageable will help you stay on top of your applications and make the college search process less overwhelming!
